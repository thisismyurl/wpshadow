MARGARET (The Host):
Let's start with the basics. In a commercial cabinet shop or millwork facility,
you're dealing with high-volume production. When we talk about sandpaper grit 
in this context, what are we actually measuring?

DAVID (The Sidekick):
It's the size of the abrasive particles on the surface. In cabinet finishing,
you're typically using grits from 80 up to 320 or 400 for final preparation 
before stain or clear coat. Finer grit numbers mean smoother particles.

JORDAN (The Help):
So in a production environment finishing maple cabinets or walnut tabletops, 
a higher number is smoother? That seems counterintuitive.

DR. PATTERSON (The Expert):
It is counterintuitive, but here's the critical part for commercial finishing: 
the numbering system refers to how many particles fit through a mesh screen per 
linear inch. So P400 has 400 particles per inch. In a cabinet shop, this matters 
because when you're finishing white oak for kitchen cabinets, you need consistent 
scratch patterns. Higher numbers mean smaller particles, which create finer 
scratches. This is essential when you're applying catalyzed lacquer or conversion 
varnish—those finishes are unforgiving and will highlight any inconsistency in 
your surface prep.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

MARGARET (The Host):
Now here's where it gets critical for commercial operations. There are actually 
two different standards in use, and many cabinet shops source materials globally. 
Can you explain those, Dr. Patterson?

DR. PATTERSON (The Expert):
Absolutely. The P-grade system comes from the FEPA standard—Federation of European 
Producers of Abrasives. The regular grade system, like 400, comes from CAMI—
Coated Abrasive Manufacturers Institute, primarily North American. In a commercial 
setting, this matters because if you're sourcing sandpaper from European suppliers 
for your wide belt sanders or stroke sanders, you need to know you're getting 
consistent grit sizing. They both measure particle size, but use slightly different 
testing methods.

JORDAN (The Help):
So if I'm running a cabinet finishing line and I order P400 from one supplier 
and 400 from another, are they interchangeable?

DAVID (The Sidekick):
That's a critical production question, because consistency determines your 
finish quality.

DR. PATTERSON (The Expert):
Here's the reality for commercial finishing: P400 and 400 are nominally equivalent, 
but P-grade has tighter tolerances. In a production environment finishing cherry 
cabinets or preparing maple tabletops for catalyzed conversion varnish, that 
precision matters. The P-grade system has a narrower particle size distribution, 
which means more consistent scratch patterns across hundreds of cabinet doors. 
With CAMI 400, you get slightly more variation. For high-end residential or 
commercial installations where the client is paying premium prices, that consistency 
is what separates acceptable from exceptional.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

MARGARET (The Host):
So in a commercial cabinet shop or millwork facility, when does this precision 
actually matter?

JORDAN (The Help):
Yeah, if I'm finishing kitchen cabinets or boardroom tables, when do I care 
about P400 versus 400?

DR. PATTERSON (The Expert):
Let me break down the three scenarios where it's critical. First, high-end 
residential kitchen cabinets in premium woods like quarter-sawn white oak or 
figured maple. These projects have tight budgets and zero tolerance for rework. 
Second, commercial installations—restaurants, hotels, corporate offices—where 
durability and appearance need to last years under heavy use. Third, tabletops 
and horizontal surfaces that will be examined closely. When you're applying 
pre-catalyzed lacquer or waterborne finishes on these surfaces, any inconsistency 
in your final sanding grit will telegraph through the finish as soon as it cures.

DAVID (The Sidekick):
So it's about the finish amplifying whatever's underneath. The better finishes 
actually make sanding mistakes more visible.

MARGARET (The Host):
Let's break down what a proper grit progression looks like in a commercial setting, 
because this is where a lot of shops lose efficiency and quality.

DR. PATTERSON (The Expert):
In a cabinet shop, your progression depends on the starting condition and your 
target finish. For raw hardwood cabinet doors—let's say red oak or maple—you 
typically start with 80 or 100 grit on your wide belt sander to remove mill marks 
and flatten the surface. Then you move to 120 to remove the 80-grit scratches. 
Then 150, which is critical because it's the last grit before you start your 
finish-prep sequence. After 150, you go to 180, then 220. For premium work with 
clear catalyzed finishes, you finish at 320 or even 400 before your stain or 
sealer coat. Each step removes the scratches from the previous grit. Skip a grit, 
and you'll have visible scratches that show through your finish—and in a 
commercial setting, that means rework, which kills your margins.
